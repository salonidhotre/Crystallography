Journey of Crystallography

Crystallography?

We know that matter is made up of atoms or groups of atoms as molecules. When these atoms or molecules are arranged in a repeating pattern it is known to be a crystal. And crystallography is an adventure of diving into the matter to study their crystal structure.

1901–

It all began when Wilhelm Roentgen was working on a cathode ray tube and discovered mysterious rays emitting from the tube. As any mathematician would use the famous variable, letter x for the unknown, this scientist named it as X-rays. Wilhelm Roentgen won the first Nobel Prize in Physics.


1914–

Nobel laureate Max Von Laue observed that these X-rays diffract when they are passed through crystals. When a thin beam of X-ray is impinged on a crystal, the reflected X-rays from atomic planes interfere to form
Laue diffraction patterns.

1915–

If these X-ray diffractions are formed by the atoms in the crystals, can we get the relative position of the atoms if we retrace the diffraction path? This thought popped up in the minds of William Henry Bragg and his son William Lawrence Bragg.
William Henry Bragg designed the X-ray spectrometer, a device that could examine the reflections of X-rays from crystals. Using simple geometry, they worked on simple crystals like NaCl, ZnS, and diamond. The father and son duo shared a Nobel prize in Physics.

Bragg’s law

Bragg’s law states that when the x-ray is incident onto a crystal surface, its angle of incidence, θ, will reflect back with the same angle of scattering, θ. And, when the path difference, d is equal to a whole number n, of wavelength, a constructive interference will occur.
PQ = AB = d (interplanar distance of atomic plane)
PS and PT are perpendicular drawn
We notice that wave 2 has to travel extra distance SQ + QT which is an integral multiple of the wavelength nλ
SQ + QT = nλ
d sin θ + d sin θ = nλ
2d sin θ = nλ
